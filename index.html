Day 1 · Oct 16

Guangzhou — Arrival Day

Arrive 12:10 from Singapore. Dim sum capital of the world.

First Day Plan

Land at Baiyun Airport at 12:10, clear customs, grab SIM cards, head to hotel. Relaxed afternoon and evening to explore before the morning train to Yangshuo.

Where We Stay

LangYue Hotel — No. 10 Fulu Street, Liwan District (Hedong East Metro). Opened 2025, clean rooms with projection screens. Alternative: Banana Fun Hotel near Ergong Metro, Haizhu District.

Dim Sum Dinner

Diǎn Dū Dé (点都德) — the most authentic har gow, siu mai, char siu bao, and rice rolls you'll taste. Budget ¥80-100/person.

Beijing Road Walking Street

500m pedestrian street with ancient ruins beneath glass panels — 2,000-year-old road foundations from the Tang Dynasty. Snacks, tea, urban energy.

Days 2–3 · Oct 17–18

Yangshuo — Karst Paradise

HSR Guangzhou 09:00 → Yangshuo 11:30

Where We Stay

West Street (Xi Jie) area — Heart of Yangshuo's traveler scene. Cobblestone streets, cafes, bars. Close to the action but expect noise at night.

Day 1 — Countryside E-bike Ride

Pedal through the Ten-Mile Gallery as karst peaks tower on both sides. Yulong River winds alongside rice paddies — ancient stone bridges, local farmers. Rent e-bike for ¥50-80/day. Best in late afternoon when light turns golden.

Day 2 — Li River Bamboo Rafting

Drift through the Xingping section — the exact landscape on the Chinese 20 yuan note. 1.5-hour ride through dramatic gorges with cormorant fishermen. Then climb Laozhai Hill (20 min, 1,500 steps) for a 360° panorama of peaks in mist.

Days 4–7 · Oct 19–22

Chengdu — Pandas, Tea & Spice

HSR from Yangshuo (5.5h)

Giant Panda Research Base

Baby pandas tumbling off logs, play-fighting siblings, napping in trees. Morning session (before 10 AM) is when cubs are most active. This alone makes Chengdu unforgettable.

People's Park & Kuanzhai Alley

Sip jasmine tea in a bamboo chair while locals practice tai chi. Wander restored Qing-dynasty lanes — snack stalls, face-changing opera performers, Sichuan pepper everything.

Chunxi Road — Neon Nights

Chengdu's Times Square. Pedestrian street packed with flagship stores, street performers, endless food. Best at night when neon signs glow.

Days 5–6 · Oct 20–21

Jiuzhaigou — Valley of Nine Villages

HSR from Chengdu (~2.5h). Peak autumn foliage.

← Swipe to explore →

Getting There (Oct 20)

Morning: People's Park tea, Kuanzhai Alley, hotpot lunch. Afternoon: HSR Chengdu → Jiuhuang (16:02–18:27). Shuttle bus or taxi (45 min) to park entrance.

Where We Stay

Lavande Hotel (Jiuzhaigou Tourist Center) — Huodiba, Longkang Village. Rating 9.5/10. Walking distance to park entrance. Bianbian Street nearby for food.

The Y-Shaped Valley Strategy (Oct 21)

Pro tip: Take shuttle to the TOP of Rize Valley first (Long Lake), walk DOWN through highlights. Saves energy, fewer crowds. Park covers 72,000 hectares — use shuttles.

Must-see route: Long Lake → Five Flower Lake (submerged trees in turquoise water) → Pearl Shoal Waterfall → Mirror Lake → Nuorilang Waterfall (China's widest) → Shuzheng Valley if time permits.

October = peak autumn foliage. Golden leaves reflecting in crystal-clear water. Arrive at 8 AM — lakes are most mirror-like before crowds stir the water.

Return (Oct 21 Evening)

Full day in park (08:00–17:00). Evening train back: HSR Jiuhuang → Chengdu (19:00–21:24).

Days 8–10 · Oct 23–25

Chongqing — The Cyberpunk City

HSR Chengdu 09:00 → Chongqing 10:30

Where We Stay

Jiefangbei / Hongyadong area — Right on top of the action. Holiday Riverview Hotel has views of Qiansimen Bridge, ~$30/night.

Day 1 — Qiansimen Bridge, Hongyadong & Jiefangbei

Qiansimen Bridge: Best elevated view of Chongqing's layered architecture — monorails, skyscrapers rising from cliffs. Hongyadong: 11-story building cascading down a cliff. Thousands of golden lanterns at night — straight out of Spirited Away. Jiefangbei: Times Square meets Shibuya.

Day 2 — Liziba, Shibati & Yangtze Cableway

Liziba: Monorail passes THROUGH a residential building. Peak Chongqing absurdity. Shibati (18 Stairs): Restored historical neighborhood — steep staircases, food alleys, views of the modern skyline. Yangtze Cableway: Vintage cable car crossing the river.

Day 3 — Wulong Karst Day Trip

Getting there: HSR to Wulong South (45 min) → shuttle to Tourist Center (45 min, ¥16). Three Natural Bridges: Colossal stone arches accessed via glass elevator. UNESCO site, Transformers 4 filming location. Walk takes 2-3 hours. Book return train early — popular slots fill fast.

Days 11–13 · Oct 26–28

Zhangjiajie — Avatar's Pandora

HSR from Chongqing (5h). The floating mountains.

← Swipe to explore →

Avatar Hallelujah Mountain

The real-life inspiration for Pandora's floating mountains. Thousand-meter sandstone pillars wrapped in clouds. James Cameron confirmed this was the visual reference. Walking among the peaks feels like another planet.

Bailong Elevator

World's tallest outdoor elevator — 326 meters up a sheer cliff face in a glass cabin. 90 seconds from valley floor to cloud-wrapped summit.

Tianmen Mountain — Heaven's Gate

World's longest cable car (7.5 km) from city center to peak. Glass skywalk bolted to cliff face 1,400m up. Descend 999 steps through the natural arch of Heaven's Gate — a massive hole through the mountain.

Ancient Towns — Choose Your Afternoon (Oct 28)

After Tianmen Mountain in the morning, we have the afternoon free. Two options — both are stunning ancient riverside towns, but very different vibes. We pick one.

Option A — Fenghuang Ancient Town

3h drive from Zhangjiajie · Best arrived by 3-4 PM for golden hour → stay for night views · Can hire a private driver (~¥400) or take the tourist bus

The "Phoenix Town" — China's most photogenic ancient settlement. Traditional Miao-style wooden stilt houses line both banks of the Tuojiang River, connected by covered bridges and stone paths. At dusk, hundreds of red lanterns switch on simultaneously and the whole town reflects in the river like a painting.

Why choose this one: Better for photography. More things to do (boat rides, night bars, Miao silver jewelry shopping). The night scene is genuinely spectacular — it's China's most-Instagrammed ancient town for a reason. Lots of food options along the river.

The tradeoff: It's popular. October is peak season. You'll share the cobblestone streets with tour groups and selfie sticks. The main strip is commercial. But walk 5 minutes off the main drag and it's quiet and beautiful.

Option B — Furong Ancient Town

2h drive from Zhangjiajie · Closer, so more flexible timing · Can combine with a late Tianmen Mountain morning

The "Hibiscus Town" — named after the 1986 Chinese film shot here. A 60-meter waterfall crashes directly through the center of town. Traditional Tujia stilt houses perch on cliff edges above the cascade. The whole town is built vertically into the rock face — stairs, not streets.

Why choose this one: More dramatic natural scenery. The waterfall-through-town thing is unique — you won't see that anywhere else in China. Significantly fewer tourists than Fenghuang. Feels more "discovery" and less "tourist attraction." The night lighting of the waterfall is haunting.

The tradeoff: Smaller town, fewer food/shopping options. Less to "do" beyond walking, photographing, and watching the waterfall. The vertical layout means lots of stairs (after a morning of 999 steps at Tianmen, your legs might file a complaint).

Current lean: Fenghuang for the night photography and river atmosphere, but Furong is closer and our legs might appreciate fewer stairs after Tianmen. We can decide on the morning of Oct 28 based on energy levels.

Eat

Food We Cannot Miss

The non-negotiable eating agenda across 4 culinary regions

Cantonese — Guangzhou Days 1, 14–16
Dim Sum (Yum Cha)
Guangzhou · Diǎn Dū Dé (点都德)
The original. Bamboo steamers stacked high with har gow (crystal shrimp dumplings), siu mai (pork dumplings), char siu bao (BBQ pork buns), cheung fun (rice noodle rolls). You point at the cart, they put it on your table. Bill is calculated by plate color. Budget ¥80-100/person for a full spread.
Har Gow (虾饺)
Guangzhou · Any dim sum restaurant
The benchmark of a good dim sum kitchen. Translucent pleated wrapper (exactly 7-10 folds if the chef is serious) filled with whole shrimp that snap when you bite. If these aren't good, leave the restaurant. They're the canary in the coal mine of Cantonese food.
Congee + Zhaliang
Guangzhou · Morning street stalls
Silky rice porridge slow-cooked until it's basically liquid velvet, served with zhaliang (fried dough wrapped in rice noodle sheet, drizzled with soy). The locals eat this at 6 AM. We'll eat it whenever we wake up. Perfect hangover food, even if the hangover is from jet lag.
Sichuan — Chengdu Days 4–7
Sichuan Hotpot
Chengdu · Literally everywhere
Split pot: one side is a churning crimson lake of chili oil, Sichuan peppercorns, and dried chilies. The other side is mild bone broth for the items you want to actually taste. You order raw ingredients (sliced meat, tofu, mushrooms, greens) and cook them yourself. The málà (numbing-spicy) side will make your lips tingle for an hour afterward. This is considered a feature.
Mapo Tofu (麻婆豆腐)
Chengdu · Chen Mapo Tofu (est. 1862)
Soft tofu cubes swimming in a fiery sauce of doubanjiang (chili bean paste), fermented black beans, ground pork, and a heavy dusting of ground Sichuan peppercorn. "Mapo" means "pockmarked grandma" — named after the woman who invented it. The original restaurant still exists in Chengdu. It's simultaneously the simplest and most complex dish on this list.
Dan Dan Noodles (担担面)
Chengdu · Street stalls, Kuanzhai Alley
Thin wheat noodles in a small bowl (this is a snack, not a meal) with a sauce made of chili oil, Sichuan pepper, minced pork, and preserved vegetables. Named after the shoulder pole ("dan dan") that street vendors used to carry them through the streets. ¥8-15 per bowl. You will eat 3.
Chongqing Style Days 8–10
Nine-Grid Hotpot (九宫格火锅)
Chongqing · Jiefangbei area
Chongqing's version is different from Sichuan — the pot is divided into 9 squares with a metal grid. Each square has different boiling intensity. Center = volcanic. Edges = gentle simmer. You place ingredients strategically based on how fast they cook. It's basically a spicy tactical game with food. The broth uses beef tallow which gives it a richer, stickier heat.
Chongqing Small Noodles (小面)
Chongqing · Any street corner, ¥10
The city's actual staple food — not hotpot, but these humble alkaline noodles in a spicy, nutty, sesame-soy sauce with pickled vegetables and peanuts. Every Chongqing person has a strong opinion about which street stall makes the best one. Breakfast food. ¥8-12. The best ones are in the most nondescript shopfronts imaginable.
Yangshuo & Street Food Days 2–3
Beer Fish (啤酒鱼)
Yangshuo · West Street restaurants
Yangshuo's signature dish. Fresh Li River fish wok-fried with local beer, tomatoes, peppers, and garlic. The beer reduces into a sweet-savory sauce that coats the crispy fish skin. Every restaurant on West Street claims theirs is "the original." None of them are. All of them are good. ¥60-80 for a whole fish.
Guilin Rice Noodles (桂林米粉)
Yangshuo & Guilin · Street stalls
Thin, silky rice noodles in a clear bone broth, topped with braised beef, pickled beans, roasted peanuts, scallions, and chili. Locals add all the condiments from the table — vinegar, chili oil, pickled peppers — until it's their perfect bowl. ¥6-10. You customize it yourself from the condiment bar. It's like Subway but for noodles and actually good.

What to Buy

Based on Reddit r/travel + r/Chinavisa recommendations

Electronics & Tech Shenzhen, Guangzhou

DJI drones — 15-20% cheaper than US/India. Flagship stores in Guangzhou and Shenzhen. Mini 4 Pro, Osmo Action 5, Pocket 3.

Xiaomi — Mi Bands, smart home gadgets at fraction of export prices. Xiaomi Home stores everywhere.

Huaqiangbei Market (Shenzhen) — World's largest electronics market. Cables, chargers, accessories.

Skip: iPhones (same price globally), laptops (Chinese keyboard layout).

Fashion & Clothing All cities

Silk — Scarves, pajamas, bedding at great prices. Chengdu and Guangzhou have good shops.

Cashmere — Inner Mongolia quality. Erdos brand is reliable. 100% cashmere sweaters.

Tailored suits — Custom in 24-48 hours. Shenzhen/Guangzhou markets copy any design.

Outdoor gear — Kailas, Toread at 30-50% of Western brands. Good for hiking gear.

Tea & Food Gifts Chengdu, Guangzhou

Chinese tea — Longjing (Dragon Well), Pu'er, Tieguanyin oolong. Buy from specialty tea shops, not tourist markets.

Sichuan peppercorns — Vacuum-sealed in Chengdu. The numbing spice that defines the cuisine.

White Rabbit Candy — Classic Chinese milk candy. Nostalgia in a wrapper. Great gifts.

Chili oil / Lao Gan Ma — Stock up on crispy chili oil. Heavy but worth it.

Culture & Souvenirs Everywhere

Calligraphy brushes — Lake Pen (Huzhou). Also ink stones and rice paper.

Jade & pearls — Only buy from certified shops. Fake jade is everywhere. Freshwater pearls are affordable.

Panda merch — Official Panda Base shop in Chengdu has quality items. Skip random tourist stalls.

Cloisonné — Traditional enamel work on copper. Beautiful and unique.

Pro tips: Bargain in markets (start at 30% asking). Use WeChat Pay for better deals. Keep receipts for expensive items. Declare electronics at customs if over ₹50,000 total.

Essentials

Practical Info

Weather in October

South (Guangzhou, Yangshuo): 20-28°C — summer clothes + light layer

Central (Chengdu, Chongqing): 15-22°C — comfortable, light jacket

Mountains (Jiuzhaigou, Zhangjiajie): 5-15°C — warm fleece + rain jacket essential

October = golden autumn. Peak foliage in mountains. Best time to visit.

Getting Around

HSR (High-Speed Rail): Book on Trip.com 15 days ahead. 2nd class is spacious (more legroom than business class flights). Trains run on the minute. Bring passport to the station.

Local transport: Didi (Chinese Uber) for taxis — works with international cards. Metro in all major cities. Keep hotel address screenshot in Chinese on phone for taxi drivers.

Airport: Baiyun (CAN) is well-connected by metro. Allow 2h before international flights.

Money & Internet

Payments: WeChat Pay / Alipay works everywhere — even street food vendors. Link international Visa/Mastercard before departure. Cash is rarely needed but keep ¥500 as backup.

Internet: China Unicom eSIM or Airalo for data. Covers all cities.

VPN: Required for Google, WhatsApp, Instagram, YouTube. Pre-install and test before departure. ExpressVPN or Astrill work best in China.

Offline prep: Download Google Maps offline for all cities. Save hotel addresses in Chinese. Download Pleco dictionary app.

FAQ

Frequently Anxious Questions

Valid concerns, mostly valid answers

Wait, neither of us speaks Mandarin?
We speak fluent "pointing at menu items" and "showing Google Translate to confused taxi drivers." WeChat has a built-in translator, every restaurant has picture menus, and the HSR system has English signage. The only Mandarin we actually need is "méi yǒu là" (no spice) and "xǐshǒujiān zài nǎlǐ" (where's the bathroom). I've been practicing both in the mirror. The pronunciation is terrible but the desperation will be convincing.
7 cities in 16 days — are we going to be exhausted?
Here's the thing — China's HSR is not like Indian railways. Chengdu to Chongqing is 90 minutes. That's shorter than our commute on ORR during rain. We're not "traveling between cities," we're sitting in a comfy recliner watching countryside blur past at 350 km/h while eating cup noodles. The trains ARE the rest days. Also I've built in a full chill day in Guangzhou at the end specifically for the "my feet are filing for divorce" phase of the trip.
No Google, no Instagram, no WhatsApp — how do we survive?
VPN is pre-installed and tested. Offline Google Maps downloaded for every city. Hotel addresses saved in Chinese screenshots. But also — and hear me out — 16 days without doom-scrolling Instagram might be the most therapeutic part of this trip. We'll be too busy watching a monorail go through a building to miss Stories. Our parents get WeChat updates; everyone else can wait for the photo dump when we're back.
Can we hold a baby panda?
They stopped allowing that in 2023. Apparently "letting tourists squeeze endangered animals" fell out of fashion. But you can stand close enough to watch baby pandas roll off a log, wrestle their siblings, and generally fail at being coordinated animals. It's like watching toddlers at a playground except they're globally endangered and covered in fur. Better content than a posed photo anyway — those videos go viral.
I can barely handle Indian spicy — what about Sichuan food?
Good news: Sichuan "spicy" is different from Indian "spicy." It's not just heat — it's this tingling numbing sensation called málà where your lips go electric. Think of it as your mouth getting a mild static shock that also tastes incredible. Every hotpot place has a split pot option: one side nuclear, one side mild broth. You control what goes in your bowl. Worst case we order the tomato base and nobody has to know.
The glass skywalk — do I actually have to do that?
You don't HAVE to. But you'll want to once you see it. It's 1,400 meters up, bolted to a cliff face, and the glass is rated for 800 people standing on it simultaneously. Structurally it's the safest path on the entire mountain. Psychologically it's an existential crisis in footwear. There are videos of grown men army-crawling across it while their girlfriends stroll casually. I have full confidence you'll be the one strolling and I'll be the one reconsidering my life choices.
What if I don't like the food somewhere?
We're crossing four distinct culinary regions. Cantonese dim sum is mild and delicate. Sichuan is the spicy one. Chongqing hotpot you control yourself. Yangshuo has beer fish and river shrimp. If all else fails — and I cannot stress this enough — there is a Luckin Coffee on every single block in every Chinese city. Also the bakeries are excellent and the fruit is absurdly cheap and fresh. You will not go hungry. The real problem will be eating too much.
How much walking are we actually talking?
My phone is going to report 15,000-20,000 steps on the big days. Jiuzhaigou and Zhangjiajie are the heavy ones but both have shuttle buses and elevators (a literal 326-meter glass elevator up a cliff). The city days are more like casual wandering with frequent dumpling stops. I've mapped every rest point, cable car, and shortcut. And if your feet stage a protest, Didi (Chinese Uber) is ¥15 to anywhere within a city. We're not proving anything to anyone.
One carry-on for 16 days? Seriously?
I'm not dragging a suitcase up 999 steps to Heaven's Gate. Here's the math: 5 outfits on rotation, laundry service at every hotel for ¥30, and China has a Miniso/convenience store within 200 meters of any point in any city. Forgot something? You can buy it. Anything we accumulate (tea, silk scarves, that DJI drone we "accidentally" buy in Shenzhen) gets shipped home or stuffed into a cheap bag for the flight back. Travel light, shop heavy.
What's the plan if something goes wrong?
Travel insurance covers medical and cancellations. Every hotel has an English-speaking front desk. I have offline copies of all bookings, passport scans, embassy numbers, and backup payment methods. But also — China is genuinely one of the safest countries to travel in. Violent crime is nearly nonexistent, public transport runs on the minute, hospitals are good, and worst case scenario the hotel can call us a car to anywhere. The most dangerous thing we'll do is try to cross a road in Chongqing where the GPS says you're on level 3 of a 5-level interchange.

This is happening. Flights booked. Hotels sorted. 16 days of ancient mountains in clouds, neon cities from sci-fi films, pandas being ridiculous, food that will ruin every other cuisine, and the two of us exploring a country that has to be seen to be believed.
